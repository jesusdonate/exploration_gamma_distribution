Applications of the Gamma Distribution

Examples of real-world situations where gamma models positive, right-skewed continuous outcomes.

1. Hospital Waiting Times

The time (in minutes) a patient waits before seeing a doctor can be modeled as \(X\sim\Gamma(\alpha,\beta)\).

\(\alpha\) gives strong right-skew (many short waits with a few very long waits), which is less predictable. Larger \(\alpha\) makes waits more predictable.

\(\beta\) controls overall size. Small \(\beta\) means smaller average waits; larger \(\beta\) means longer average waits.

In practice, larger \(\alpha\) and smaller \(\beta\) are preferred.

Dataset: Kaggle ER Wait Time

2. Insurance Claim Sizes

Claim amounts are positive and often right-skewed, making gamma a useful model.

\(\alpha\) influences tail behavior and claim variability.

\(\beta\) scales claim severity and affects expected payout.

Higher \(\alpha\) with lower \(\beta\) often implies more stable and lower average claim costs.

Dataset: Minitab Insurance Fraud Data

3. Rainfall Intensity

Rainfall amount per interval can be modeled using gamma when values are positive and skewed.

\(\alpha\) reflects consistency of storms; smaller values suggest more irregular rainfall.

\(\beta\) controls overall scale; larger values correspond to heavier rainfall levels.

Different climates can be compared by estimated \((\alpha,\beta)\) values.

Dataset: Kaggle Rainfall Dataset
