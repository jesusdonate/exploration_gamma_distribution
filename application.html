Applications of the Gamma Distribution

Examples of real-world situations where gamma models positive, right-skewed continuous outcomes.

1. Hospital Waiting Times

The time (in minutes) a patient waits before seeing a doctor can be modeled as \(X\sim\Gamma(\alpha,\beta)\).

Small \(\alpha\) gives strong right-skew (many short waits with a few very long waits), which is less predictable. Larger \(\alpha\) makes waits more predictable.

Small \(\beta\) means smaller average waits and larger \(\beta\) means longer average waits.

In practice, larger \(\alpha\) and smaller \(\beta\) are preferred.

Dataset: Kaggle ER Wait Time

2. Insurance Claim Sizes

Claim amounts are positive and often right-skewed, making gamma a useful model.

Small \(\alpha\) means the distribution has a strong right skew, so claims can be harder to predict since there is a high probability of large claims. Large \(\alpha\) means the distribution has a more symmertic shape to it. Claims are more predictable.

Small \(\beta\) means lower average payouts and larger \(\beta\) means higher average payouts.

Higher \(\alpha\) with lower \(\beta\) often implies more stable and lower average claim costs.

Dataset: Minitab Insurance Fraud Data

3. Rainfall Intensity

Rainfall amount per interval can be modeled using gamma when values are positive and skewed.

Small \(\alpha\) means the distribution has a strong right skew, so claims can be harder to predict since there is a high probability of large claims. Large \(\alpha\) means the distribution has a more symmertic shape to it. Claims are more predictable.

Small \(\beta\) means lower average payouts and larger \(\beta\) means higher average payouts.

The best parameter values depend on the region; e.g. in a desert, we would prefer a smaller \(\alpha\), since many days have zero or small traces of rain. But in rainforests, the opposite is true.

Dataset: Kaggle Rainfall Dataset
